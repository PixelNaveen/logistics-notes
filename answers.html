Question 1 — Logistics Management & Challenges (25 marks)

a) Definition + significance (10) • b) Three key challenges (15)
a) Definition (concise):

Logistics management is the planning, implementation and control of the efficient, effective forward and reverse flow and storage of goods, services and related information between the point of origin and the point of consumption to meet customer requirements.

Significance (structured):
  1. Customer service & responsiveness: Ensures the right product, quantity, place and time (the "7Rs"), improving satisfaction and retention.
  2. Cost control: Reduces transport, inventory and handling costs to improve profitability.
  3. Supply chain integration: Coordinates partners to reduce delays and the bullwhip effect.
  4. Competitive advantage: Fast and reliable logistics (e.g., same-day delivery) differentiates firms.
  5. Adaptation to disruptions: Uses information systems and flexible networks to respond to shocks (e.g., pandemics, port congestion).
Short example:

A shipping company that synchronizes ETAs with trucking and warehousing reduces port dwell time and improves lead times.

b) Three key challenges:
  1. Supply chain disruptions & uncertainty: Natural disasters, geopolitics and pandemics cause lead-time variability and stockouts. Managers should diversify suppliers and create contingency plans.
  2. Rising transportation & energy costs: Fuel price volatility raises delivered costs. Managers can consolidate shipments and optimize modes.
  3. Digitalization & data integration: Legacy systems and siloed data reduce visibility. Invest in TMS/WMS, APIs and shared KPIs to improve decisions.

Question 2 — EOQ Calculation & Commentary (25 marks)

a) EOQ model (10) • b) Numerical EOQ (10) • c) Pros & cons (5)
a) EOQ concept & formula:

EOQ finds the order quantity Q* that minimizes total annual inventory cost (ordering + holding):

Q* = √(2 D Co / Ch)

Variables: D = annual demand, Co = ordering cost, Ch = holding cost per unit/year.

Assumptions: constant demand and lead time, whole-order deliveries, constant unit cost, known ordering & holding costs.

b) Numerical calculation (worked):

Given: D=2000 units/year, unit cost= $2000 → holding rate 1% → Ch= $20, Co= $200.

Q* = √(2×2000×200 / 20) = √(40,000) = 200 units.

c) Advantages & disadvantages:
  • Advantages: Simple, minimizes ordering+holding cost, good baseline for stable items.
  • Disadvantages: Assumes constant demand/lead time; ignores quantity discounts and service-level targets; not suited for perishables.

Question 3 — JIT, Lean Wastes & Risks (25 marks)

a) JIT principles (10) • b) 4 wastes (10) • c) Implementation risks (5)
a) JIT principles:
  1. Produce only what is needed, when needed, in the exact quantity.
  2. Pull-based production (Kanban signals).
  3. Waste minimization (reduce inventory, waiting, defects).
  4. Continuous improvement (Kaizen).
  5. Close supplier relationships for frequent, reliable deliveries.
b) Four common wastes (brief):
  1. Overproduction: creates excess inventory and ties capital.
  2. Waiting: idle time for people/machines reduces throughput.
  3. Transport: unnecessary movement increases cost and damage risk.
  4. Defects: rework and scrap add cost and delay.
c) Challenges/risks of JIT:
  • Supplier reliability — failures cause stoppages.
  • Demand variability — spikes cause stockouts.
  • Cultural change & implementation cost.
  • Low buffers increase vulnerability to transport or supply shocks.

Question 4 — Cross-docking & AS/RS (25 marks)

a) Cross-docking & requirements (10) • b) AS/RS advantages & scenarios (10) • c) Warehouse layout factors (5)
a) Cross-docking:

Cross-docking moves incoming shipments directly to outbound trucks with minimal storage (often <24 hours) to reduce holding and handling costs.

Requirements: accurate WMS/TMS, dock scheduling, reliable supplier ETAs, efficient material handling layout, pre-assigned destinations.

b) AS/RS advantages & scenarios:
  • High space efficiency and vertical utilization.
  • Higher accuracy and fewer picking errors.
  • Faster throughput and 24/7 operation capability.
  • Reduced labor cost and improved safety.

Scenarios: cold storage, e-commerce micro-fulfillment, high-value electronics, pharmaceuticals.

c) Key layout factors:
  1. Flow of goods (separate receiving from shipping).
  2. Product slotting (fast movers near shipping).
  3. Balance space utilization vs accessibility.
  4. Material handling suitability and safety.
  5. Scalability for peaks.

Question 5 — Storage, Racking & Inventory Role (25 marks)

a) 4 selection factors (4) • b) Selective vs double-deep racking (5) • c) Inventory role (8) • d) Warehouse process (8)
a) Four factors to consider:
  1. SKU characteristics (size, weight, fragility).
  2. Throughput & turnover rate.
  3. Space & ceiling height.
  4. Operational cost and labour availability.
b) Selective vs double-deep racking:

Selective racking: every pallet is directly accessible — maximum selectivity but lower space utilization. Double-deep: stores two pallets deep for higher density but reduced selectivity and needs reach trucks.

c) Role of inventory management:
  1. Balancing service level vs holding cost.
  2. Buffering variability (safety stock).
  3. Enabling continuous production and fulfillment.
  4. Providing data visibility and control via WMS/perpetual systems.
  5. Improving cash flow via turnover metrics.
d) Warehouse process (inbound → outbound):
  1. Receiving (inspect & record).
  2. Unloading & staging.
  3. Inspection & put-away (WMS updates).
  4. Storage (FIFO/FEFO as needed).
  5. Order picking (zone/wave/batch).
  6. Packing & consolidation.
  7. Staging for dispatch & loading.
  8. Returns handling (reverse logistics).

Question 6 — Reverse Logistics & Humanitarian Logistics (25 marks)

a) Reverse logistics (10) • b) Traditional vs humanitarian logistics (10) • c) NGO response plan (5)
a) Reverse logistics:

Reverse logistics moves goods back from final destination for value recovery, repair, recycling, or proper disposal. Examples: returns, recalls, e-waste recycling, warranty repairs. Benefits include value recovery, sustainability, better customer service and regulatory compliance.

b) Traditional vs humanitarian logistics:

Traditional focuses on commercial delivery and profit; humanitarian focuses on rapid life-saving delivery with unpredictable demand, damaged infrastructure, mixed donor supplies, and complex coordination. Challenges: access & security, rapid scale-up, coordination, information gaps and accountability.

c) NGO response steps (concise plan):
  1. Rapid needs assessment & coordinate with local authorities and clusters.
  2. Activate pre-positioned stocks & contingency plans.
  3. Transportation: assess access routes, prioritize airlift if roads damaged, contract carriers.
  4. Set up temporary warehousing & staging areas near affected zones.
  5. Distribution & last-mile: use local partners, register beneficiaries and ensure fair distribution.
  6. Track and report supplies for donor accountability.
