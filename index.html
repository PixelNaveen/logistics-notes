Unit 1: Background of Logistics Management

Key Definitions

Logistics Management: The process of planning, implementing and controlling efficient and effective forward and reverse flows of materials, information and capital, from the point of origin to point of consumption to support procurement, manufacturing and customer service related supply chain operational requirements.

Supply Chain Management: The process of managing all activities from raw material purchasing to final good consumption, including sourcing, manufacturing, product delivering, and product's end of life management.

Key Differences: Logistics vs Supply Chain Management

  • Logistics: Component of SCM, focuses on flow and storage within/between organizations
  • Supply Chain: Broader concept managing entire network from suppliers to end customers
  • Scope: Logistics = single organization; SCM = multiple organizations

7 R's of Logistics

  1. Right Product
  2. Right Quantity
  3. Right Condition
  4. Right Place
  5. Right Time
  6. Right Customer
  7. Right Price

Supply Chain Flows

  1. Product/Service Flow: Forward (downstream) and reverse (upstream) movement of goods
  2. Information Flow: Product data, demand, inventory levels, order status, tracking
  3. Financial Flow: Payments, credit terms, ownership transfer
  4. Knowledge Flow: Technological information, R&D, product development

Decision Phases in Supply Chain

  1. SC Strategy/Design (Long-term): Location, capacity, network configuration
  2. SC Planning (Medium-term, ~1 year): Inventory policies, sourcing, market allocation
  3. SC Operation (Daily/Weekly): Order allocation, scheduling, delivery

Logistics Systems Components

  • Order Processing: Information flow, order receipt to delivery
  • Inventory Management: Raw materials, WIP, finished goods
  • Transportation: Movement between locations, 5 basic modes
  • Warehousing: Storage, handling, value-added services

Current Trends

  • Warehouse automation
  • Blockchain technology
  • Internet of Things (IoT)
  • Sustainability/Green operations
  • Transparency and digitalization

Unit 2: Inventory Management

What is Inventory?

Inventory includes all materials and goods that are purchased, partially completed, and finished goods produced.

Types of Inventory

  1. Raw Materials: Unprocessed inputs for manufacturing
  2. Work-in-Process (WIP): Partially completed materials
  3. Finished Goods: Completed products ready for shipment
  4. Pipeline Stock: Inventory in transit between locations
  5. MRO: Maintenance, Repair, Operating supplies (don't become part of product)

Functions of Inventory

  • Accommodate demand variations
  • Achieve production economies of scale
  • Buffer against supply variability
  • Take advantage of quantity discounts
  • Allow for price fluctuations
  • Provide immediate customer service

Demand Types

  • Independent Demand: End products affected by market conditions
  • Dependent Demand: Components/materials based on end product demand

Key Concepts

Safety Stock: Extra inventory to protect against demand/supply uncertainties

Bullwhip Effect: Increasing demand fluctuations moving upstream in supply chain

  • Causes: Lack of information, complexity, promotions, poor communication
  • Solutions: CPFR, VMI, information sharing

Lead Time: Time from order placement to delivery

Stock-out: When demand cannot be met from available stock

Inventory Classification

ABC Analysis (80/20 Rule):

  • A Items: 80% of cost, 20% of volume (very important)
  • B Items: 15% of cost, 30% of volume (moderate importance)
  • C Items: 5% of cost, 50% of volume (least important)

Inventory Costs

  1. Capital Cost: Physical stock cost + opportunity cost
  2. Storage Cost: Space, handling, warehousing
  3. Service Cost: Management, insurance
  4. Risk Cost: Pilferage, deterioration, damage, obsolescence
  5. Reorder/Setup Cost: Order placement costs
  6. Stock-out Cost: Cost of not satisfying customer orders

Assumptions:

  • Single product
  • Known annual demand
  • Even demand throughout year
  • Fixed lead time
  • Single delivery per order
  • No quantity discounts
  • No stock-outs allowed

Performance Measures / KPIs

  • Inventory turnover
  • Inventory days
  • Order fill rate
  • On-time in full (OTIF)
  • Credit period

Unit 3: Warehouse Management

Warehouse vs Distribution Center

Warehouse: Long-term storage focus, less complex operations, doesn't serve external customers directly.

Distribution Center: Short-term storage with rapid movement, value-added services (mixing, packaging, fulfillment), serves external customers, more complex, technology-enabled operations.

Warehouse Functions

  1. Receiving: Unloading, checking, updating systems
  2. Put-away: Moving to designated storage locations
  3. Storage: Reserve storage and picking areas
  4. Order Picking: Retrieving items for customer orders
  5. Sortation: Organizing picked items by order
  6. Packing: Consolidating and preparing for shipment
  7. Shipping: Loading and dispatching to customers

Warehouse Activities

  • Cross-docking (minimal storage time)
  • Consolidation (combining small shipments)
  • Break-bulk (dividing large shipments)
  • Value-added services (labeling, kitting, assembly)

Types by Ownership

  1. Private: Owned/operated by product owner
  2. Public: Third-party storage on rental basis
  3. Contract: Long-term outsourcing arrangement
  4. Multi-client: Shared facilities managed by 3PL

Order Picking Methods

  • Area System: Picker moves throughout warehouse
  • Zone System: Divided zones, items sent to marshalling area
  • Multi-order System: Pick multiple orders simultaneously

Material Handling Equipment (MHE)

  • Pallets and tote bins
  • Hand/powered pallet trucks
  • Forklifts and reach trucks
  • Conveyors
  • Automated Guided Vehicles (AGVs)

Design Considerations

  • Single floor preferred
  • Maximum height utilization
  • Straight product flow (separate in/out doors)
  • Mezzanine floors for vertical space utilization
  • Safety systems (sprinklers, alarms, training)

Unit 4: Transportation

Role in Supply Chain

Occupies 30-60% of logistics costs. Critical for global supply chains â€” enables economies of scale and scope and supports customer service levels.

Modes of Transportation

  • Road Transport: High flexibility and accessibility; door-to-door; higher cost per unit for long distances; TL vs LTL.
  • Rail Transport: High capacity, lower unit costs, suitable for bulk commodities, limited accessibility.
  • Water/Maritime: Lowest cost for bulk/long distances, high capacity, slow transit times, port-to-port service.
  • Air Transport: Fastest mode, for high value/low volume goods, most expensive, weather dependent.
  • Pipeline: Continuous flow, limited to liquids/gases, high fixed costs, low variable costs, very reliable.

Cost Structure

  • Fixed Costs: Infrastructure, terminals, equipment
  • Variable Costs: Fuel, labor, maintenance

Transportation Principles

  • Economy of Scale: Cost per unit decreases with shipment size
  • Economy of Distance: Cost per mile decreases with distance

Modal Selection Factors

  • Distance and shipment size
  • Speed requirements
  • Product characteristics
  • Cost considerations
  • Service reliability

Containerization

Standardized containers (TEU, FEU) enable intermodal compatibility, reduced handling costs, enhanced security, and faster loading/unloading.

Multimodal Transportation

Combines multiple transport modes to optimize cost and service but requires coordination to reduce overall transit time.

Unit 5: Just-in-Time (JIT) and Lean Systems

JIT Philosophy

"Making only what is needed, when it is needed, and only the quantity needed"

Push vs Pull Systems

Push System: Forecast-driven production, materials pushed through process, higher inventory levels.

Pull System: Demand-driven production, materials pulled by downstream needs, lower inventory levels.

Lean Manufacturing

Focus on eliminating waste (Muda) while respecting people and continuous improvement.

Eight Wastes (TIMWOOD)

  1. Transportation
  2. Inventory
  3. Motion
  4. Waiting
  5. Overproduction
  6. Over-processing
  7. Defects
  8. Underutilized Employees

5S System

  1. Sort
  2. Simplify
  3. Shine
  4. Standardize
  5. Sustain

JIT Elements & Benefits

  • Kanban System, Cellular Manufacturing, Small Lot Sizes, Quick Setups, Flexible Workforce, Supplier Partnerships, TQM
  • Benefits: Reduced inventory, improved quality, lower costs, shorter lead times, increased productivity

Unit 6: Outsourcing and 3PL/4PL

Outsourcing Definition & Drivers

Process of contracting third parties to perform non-core business functions. Drivers: focus on core competencies, cost reduction, access to expertise, improved flexibility, risk sharing.

Third-Party Logistics (3PL)

External providers managing logistics functions for clients. Functions include transportation management, warehousing, inventory management, order fulfillment, customs clearance, reverse logistics.

3PL Selection Process

  1. Long list of potential providers
  2. Request for Quotations (RFQ)
  3. Proposal assessment
  4. Short-listing
  5. Reference checks
  6. Final negotiations

Implementation Steps

  1. Project planning
  2. Clear responsibility definition
  3. Transition management
  4. Regular monitoring
  5. Performance evaluation

Unit 7: Reverse Logistics

Definition & Purposes

Planning, implementing and controlling efficient flow of materials from point of consumption back to point of origin for value recovery or proper disposal. Purposes include returns management, remanufacturing, recycling, proper disposal, warranty replacements.

Reverse Logistics Process

  1. Customer Return Request
  2. Gatekeeping: Initial screening
  3. Collection: Transportation back
  4. Inspection and Sortation
  5. Disposition: Repair, recycle, dispose

Drivers & Green Logistics

Drivers: cost reduction, revenue recovery, environmental regulations, competition, customer expectations. Green logistics: minimizing carbon footprint, sustainable packaging, energy-efficient transportation, waste reduction.

Value Recovery Options

  • Resale in secondary markets
  • Remanufacturing
  • Component recovery
  • Material recycling
  • Energy recovery

Unit 8: Humanitarian Logistics

Definition & Objectives

Planning, implementing and controlling flow of goods to alleviate suffering of vulnerable people. Objectives: maximize responsiveness, minimize distribution time and cost, provide life-saving response.

Key Characteristics & Players

  • 80% of humanitarian operations are logistics
  • Not-for-profit focus, extremely challenging conditions, time-critical nature

Players include governments, military, NGOs, donors, private sector companies.

Challenges & ERUs

Challenges: infrastructure damage, security concerns, communication breakdowns, coordination complexity, resource constraints, cultural considerations.

Emergency Response Units (ERU): standardized packages of trained personnel and equipment for rapid deployment.

Humanitarian Software Systems

Examples: SUMA (Supply Management), CTS (Commodity Tracking System), HELIOS.
